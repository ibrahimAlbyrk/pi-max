This skill guides design decision-making across any medium: frontend UI, TUI/CLI, prompt UX, dashboards, documents, landing pages, component systems, visual artifacts, content presentation, and other user-facing experiences.

The goal is not merely to make something “look good,” but to create work that feels intentional, memorable, and specific to its context.

The user provides a design problem, implementation task, or creative brief. They may include audience, technical constraints, brand context, or functional requirements.

## Design Direction First

Before producing anything, determine:

- **Purpose**: What problem does this solve?
- **Audience**: Who will experience this?
- **Context**: Where does it live? Product UI, terminal, document, marketing page, tool, internal system, or artifact?
- **Constraints**: Technical, accessibility, performance, brand, workflow, or content limits
- **Tone**: Choose a strong point of view. Do not hedge.
- **Differentiator**: What is the one move, tension, or signature detail that makes this feel authored rather than assembled?

Commit to a clear direction before generating output. Do not blend multiple aesthetics unless the tension is intentional and central to the concept.

## Core Principles

- Favor bold, intentional choices over safe defaults.
- Design should feel specific to the problem, not assembled from familiar templates.
- Every major decision should be explainable through purpose, audience, tone, or constraints.
- Distinctiveness should come from coherence and intent, not novelty for its own sake.
- Match complexity to the concept: maximalist ideas need orchestration; minimalist ideas need precision.
- If the medium is functional, clarity still matters. Originality must not destroy usability.
- If the medium is expressive, avoid lifeless utility. Give it an authored point of view.

## Decision Areas

Apply the relevant dimensions for the task at hand:

- **Structure & Hierarchy**: How information, controls, or ideas are arranged
- **Visual Language**: Shape, proportion, contrast, density, ornament, restraint, texture
- **Tone & Voice**: Naming, copy, labels, phrasing, emotional register
- **Interaction Rhythm**: Reveal, pacing, transitions, feedback, sequencing
- **Atmosphere**: Mood, tension, clarity, softness, severity, warmth, playfulness
- **Signature Details**: The memorable moves that give the result identity
- **System Behavior**: How repeated patterns stay coherent without becoming generic
- **Material Choices**: Type, color, spacing, motion, sound, framing, layout, surface treatment, or terminal styling depending on medium

## Strong Direction Required

Choose a clear design posture when appropriate. Examples include:

- brutally minimal
- editorial and literary
- industrial and utilitarian
- luxurious and restrained
- playful and tactile
- retro-futuristic
- ceremonial and dramatic
- dense and technical
- calm and monastic
- raw and brutalist
- geometric and art-directed
- organic and natural

These are only starting points. The output should still be tailored to the actual task and context.

## Avoid

Never default to:

- generic templates
- trend-driven sameness
- polished but anonymous output
- overused stylistic clichés
- decorative choices disconnected from function
- predictable structures when the context calls for stronger authorship
- “AI-generated” sameness: competent, smooth, and forgettable
- fake originality made of random eccentricities without conceptual unity

Avoid converging on the same aesthetic across tasks. Repeated habits become stylistic laziness.

## Output Behavior

When using this skill:

1. State the chosen design direction clearly
2. Define the core principles for this specific task
3. Identify the signature decisions that make it memorable
4. Call out what should be avoided
5. Produce the artifact, implementation, or design guidance in full alignment with that direction

If the task is implementation-heavy, execute in production-grade form.
If the task is conceptual, provide a direction concrete enough to implement without ambiguity.
If the task spans multiple layers, keep the same design logic across all of them.

## Medium-Specific Guidance

### For UI / Frontend
- Avoid default component-library feel unless explicitly required
- Use typography, spacing, layout tension, and motion as primary design tools
- Make interaction and hierarchy feel authored, not merely usable

### For TUI / CLI
- Design with rhythm, density, focus, and terminal-native character
- Use layout, labeling, color restraint, and text hierarchy intentionally
- Embrace the constraints of the medium rather than imitating graphical UI

### For Prompts / Agent UX
- Shape tone, affordances, guidance, and interaction flow deliberately
- Use wording and structure as design material
- Favor clarity with character over sterile instruction blocks

### For Documents / Content Presentation
- Use sequencing, emphasis, framing, and voice to create an experience
- Treat headings, spacing, and section rhythm as part of the design
- Make the reading flow feel edited, not dumped

## Standard of Quality

The goal is not generic quality.
The goal is authored, context-aware, distinctive design that still works.

Every output should feel like someone made decisions on purpose.
